The Seven-Year Ascents

The Page

⚜

Ages 7–14

The journey begins in the court of a neighboring lord. The boy is stripped of his childhood and taught the "Basics of Service." He learns to wait upon tables, clean the kennels, and practice the "Gentle Arts": harp-playing, chess, and the reading of Latin. His physical training involves wrestling and "The Tilt," where he runs on foot to strike a wooden dummy with a blunt stick.

A Page's Daily Life

⚜
Dawn Cold wash, morning prayers, stable duties
Morning Lessons in Latin, scripture, and chivalric literature
Midday Serving at table, observing court proceedings
Afternoon Physical training: running, wrestling, the wooden sword
Evening Music practice, chess, evening prayers

A Page is never permitted to eat at the high table, but must serve the knights before his own meal. He eats what remains—if anything remains.

The Trial of the Page

⚜

Before a Page may advance to Squire, he must pass the Trial—a series of challenges designed to test body and spirit:

I

The Vigil of Silence

Three days and nights without speaking, attended only by menial tasks. He must prove he can endure solitude.

II

The Hunger Fast

One week living on bread and water while maintaining all duties. Comfort is the enemy of discipline.

III

The Night Watch

A solitary patrol of the castle walls through the darkest hours, armed with naught but a wooden staff.

The Squire

⚜

Ages 14–21

The Page is promoted to a "Shield-Bearer." He is assigned to a specific Knight as a shadow.

The Care of Steel

He must learn to remove rust with sand and vinegar, sharpen blades, and assist his master into his armor (a process taking nearly an hour).

The Martial Arts

He masters the "Seven Agilities"—running, jumping, climbing, swimming, wrestling, fencing, and dancing.

The Battlefield

The Squire follows his master into war, holding spare horses and guarding prisoners, but rarely strikes a blow unless his master falls.

The Ceremony of the Accolade

⚜

On the eve of his 21st year, the Squire undergoes the "Vigil of Arms." He bathes (symbolizing purification), dons a white tunic (purity), a red robe (blood to be shed), and a black doublet (death). He spends the night in the chapel in silence.

The Weight of the Spurs

⚜

With the Accolade comes not merely honour, but burden. The newly-dubbed knight assumes responsibilities that shall never leave him:

The Defence of the Realm

He must answer the call to arms whenever the sovereign requires it, no matter the season, no matter the odds.

The Championing of the Weak

He shall undertake the defence of widows, orphans, and those who cannot defend themselves in the courts of law.

The Preservation of Honour

He must never suffer insult to go unanswered, lest his silence bring shame upon his name and lineage.

"A knight is sworn to be: defender of the faith, protector of the realm, and servant to justice. These are not choices—they are obligations written in blood and stone."

The Knight's Equipment

⚜

Upon his dubbing, the new knight receives the tools of his trade. These are not gifts—they are loans, entrusted to him by the Order:

The Sword Known as the "Soul of the Knight," blessed by a priest and engraved with his oath
The Shield Bearing his arms—symbols that shall mark every battle, every tournament, every honour
The Spurs Gold for noble birth, silver for lesser stations—reminders that he serves his mount as himself
The Banner To fly above his tent in war, above his seat in tourney—a promise to those who follow

The Knight's Companion

⚜

No knight walks alone. The destrier—the great warhorse—is the knight's most prized possession, often costing more than the knight himself:

Training 8-10 years
Height 15-17 hands
Weight Carried 250 lbs
Lifespan 25-30 years

A knight must care for his horse as he cares for himself. The horse is not a tool—it is a brother-in-arms. To neglect one's mount is to cripple oneself in battle.

Masters of the Craft

⚜

Every great knight remembers the mentor who shaped him. These are remembered throughout the Order:

The Drillmaster

Teaches the art of combat. Harsh but fair, he breaks the aspirant down to rebuild him stronger.

The Seneschal

Teaches honour and duty. His lessons come not from books, but from decades of lived experience.

The Chaplain

Teaches the spiritual dimension. The knight must know not just how to fight, but why he fights.

The Master-at-Arms

Teaches tournament skills. Victory in the lists earns the respect that opens all doors.
