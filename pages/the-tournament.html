The Carnival of War

The Lists and the Grandstand

⚜

The tournament is the "War Game." It is where a landless knight can win a fortune in ransoms or a wealthy knight can lose his reputation in a single heartbeat.

The Anatomy of the Joust

⚜

The joust is more than two knights charging at each other. It is a precise art with strict rules:

The Charge

Knights begin at opposite ends of the lists, some 80 yards apart. At the herald's cry, they spur their mounts forward.

The Impact

Lances must be held underarm, pointed at the opponent's shield. A clean strike to the "point of the shield" earns a point.

The Splinter

The best strikes shatter the lance spectacularly. The crowd judges the quality of each exchange.

The Unhorsing

The ultimate victory: to strike your opponent from his saddle. No greater honour exists on the field.

Champions of Legend

⚜

These warriors carved their names into the annals of tournament history:

Sir William Marshal

Over fifty tournaments fought, never once unhorsed. He jousted into his seventies, proving that honour knows no age.

The Black Knight

A mystery knight who appeared at tournament after tournament under a black helm. His true identity was never discovered, though many claimed him.

Sir Henry the Hallow

Never raised his lance to strike an opponent who had shown him mercy. Honour above victory was his creed.

The Joust

The "Main Event." Knights charge at 20 miles per hour, aiming their 12-foot lances at the "Small Plate" of the opponent's left shoulder. To splinter a lance is a point; to unhorse the opponent is a victory.

The Mêlée

Two companies of knights charge each other at once. It is a chaotic, bruising brawl designed to test tactical cohesion and stamina.

The Pas d'Armes

A "Passage of Arms" where a single knight (The Tenans) holds a bridge or crossroad against all comers (The Venans) for a set number of days.

The Prize

⚜

It is not merely gold. The victor often wins the armor and horse of the defeated, or the "Crown of Roses" to bestow upon a lady of the court.

The Tournament Court

⚜

Beyond the combat, tournaments were grand social occasions where alliances were forged and fortunes exchanged:

The Feast

Three days of feasting accompany any major tournament. Knights display their prowess; ladies judge their bearing.

The Ball

Dancing continues until dawn, where knights may earn favour through grace rather than the lance.

The Challenge Board

A board where knights post challenges, taunts, and replies—early trash talk written for all to read.

Tournament Armaments

⚜

Unlike battle weapons, tournament arms are designed for spectacle and safety, though safety was often relative:

The Rounceval Lance

Thick and hollow, designed to shatter spectacularly on impact

The Shield of Registration

Painted with the knight's arms, used to judge accurate strikes

The Corned Helmet

Reinforced headgear designed to withstand the impact of lance strikes

The Barbed Lance

Bitter and barbed—removed after the tournament to prevent reuse

The Laws of the Lists

⚜

A tournament without law becomes a massacre. These are the immutable rules:

I

No knight may strike below the opponent's belt, upon a fallen knight, or after the yielding cry

II

All lances must be measured by the marshal before engagement—none may exceed the agreed length

III

The herald's command is absolute. To charge before the signal is to forfeit all honour

IV

Captured knights are ransomed—not slain. The tournament is war's rehearsal, not war itself

V

Lies against another knight's honour may be answered only with steel, not words
